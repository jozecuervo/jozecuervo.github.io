# Screen Time Is the Wrong Question

A school screen-time survey recently asked a reasonable-sounding question: how much screen time is appropriate during the school day?

The immediate context is bigger than one school. The Los Angeles Times reported on April 21, 2026 that Los Angeles Unified had approved a sharp reversal in classroom technology policy: no screen access for kindergarten and first grade, tracking and limits for older students, restrictions involving platforms such as YouTube, Roblox, and Fortnite, a push toward more paper-and-pen work, possible returns to laptop carts or computer labs, and a broader cost-benefit review of classroom technology contracts.[^latimes-lausd]

I understand the reaction. Parents and educators are right to worry about passive video, games, ads, autoplay, algorithmic recommendations, privacy, attention, and the degree to which screens have replaced hands-on work after years of pandemic-era device expansion. But if the answer is only "less screen time," we risk making the same category error in the opposite direction.

The LAUSD article itself makes the distinction visible. Much of the harm research is about out-of-school use: phones, games, scrolling, social media, and video. Yet the policy debate now reaches schoolwork screens too. That is where precision matters. Limiting YouTube autoplay, ad exposure, games, recess device use, and passive digital worksheets is a different problem from teaching students to type, write, research, code, make art, compose music, analyze data, and use AI responsibly.

My answer, if forced into the available choices, is: **more than two hours per day can be appropriate**. In some cases, it may be desirable.

That answer only makes sense if we stop treating "screen time" as one category. A child using a laptop to write, research, type, code, make music, edit images, analyze data, create a presentation, work with accessibility tools, or revise a draft with an AI assistant is doing something fundamentally different from a child scrolling an algorithmic feed on a phone.

The screen is not the issue by itself. The issue is whether the screen is being used as an instrument or as a slot machine.

## My Bias

I am not anti-technology. My media and technology experience reaches back to print news, television, film, and software production in the era of floppy disks, then through publishing, instant messaging, social media, social gaming, streaming media, and programmatic advertising platforms from roughly 1998 through the present. I have seen media shift from physical production and broadcast schedules into websites, messaging clients, social graphs, recommendation systems, mobile apps, ad exchanges, streaming platforms, and now AI-assisted workflows.

That background makes me more optimistic about children learning real computing skills, not less. It also makes me more cautious about putting children inside systems designed primarily around engagement, ranking, notifications, social comparison, advertising, and data capture.

Social gaming was especially clarifying. Some products in that era were designed around compulsive loops and the monetization of addictive tendencies. Social media, short-form video, mobile games, and ad-supported platforms learned many of the same lessons. When adults talk about "screen time" as if Photoshop, GarageBand, Google Docs, TikTok, a typing tutor, YouTube Shorts, a code editor, and a mobile game are comparable, we are flattening the part that matters most.

## The Better Taxonomy

A useful school technology policy should distinguish at least these categories:

1. **Creative computing.** Writing, coding, music production, image editing, video editing, digital art, robotics, CAD, spreadsheets, presentations, simulations, data analysis, and project work.

2. **AI-assisted learning and authorship.** Age-appropriate use of AI for brainstorming, revision, critique, source comparison, accessibility, debugging, and reflection, with clear expectations around disclosure and verification.

3. **General-purpose computer fluency.** Typing, file management, folders, browser tabs, keyboard shortcuts, copy/paste discipline, search quality, citations, passwords, privacy settings, version history, and basic troubleshooting.

4. **Passive media.** Digital television, streaming video, and long-form viewing. This can be fine in bounded contexts, but it should be counted separately from creative work.

5. **Algorithmic engagement products.** Infinite scroll, autoplay, public likes, streaks, recommendation loops, push notifications, targeted feeds, and products optimized around time-on-platform.

6. **Social media and unsupervised communication.** Messaging with strangers, public posting, follower metrics, social ranking, adult contact, weak identity boundaries, and permanent public records.

These are not equivalent. A student composing music on a laptop is not having the same experience as a student alone with a phone. A student using AI to compare drafts and check sources is not doing the same thing as a student watching autoplay video. A shared classroom screen is not the same risk profile as a private notification-driven device in a pocket.

## The Smallest Screens Often Carry the Largest Risk

One reason the "screen time" metric is misleading is that the smallest screens often create the worst incentives.

A phone is portable, private, interruptive, camera-equipped, socially connected, notification-driven, and usually optimized around apps. An app-first tablet can share many of those properties. A television is also screen time, and should count, but it is more visible, more shared, and easier to bound. A desktop or laptop with a keyboard, files, creative software, and adult visibility is a different environment again.

The American Academy of Pediatrics has moved away from one-size-fits-all hour limits and toward a more contextual view of media use: content, context, balance, sleep, relationships, and family expectations matter.[^aap-screen] Its newer digital-ecosystem framing also distinguishes educational and creative uses from engagement-based design risks.[^aap-digital]

That matches what parents can observe directly. A clock alone cannot tell us whether technology is helping a child become more capable or training the child to be a passive consumer of an attention marketplace.

## Social Media Is a Separate Category

I would draw a much harder line around social media than around computers.

That does not mean every concern should become a government ban. Parents, schools, platforms, and policymakers all have different roles. But from a parent's perspective, I do not think early access to social media is in a child's best interest. Social media combines identity, public performance, peer ranking, adult contact, private messaging, advertising, permanence, and algorithmic amplification in ways that are different from a word processor, a spreadsheet, a drawing app, a coding environment, or a music tool.

The U.S. Surgeon General's 2023 advisory concluded that we cannot yet say social media is sufficiently safe for children and adolescents, and highlighted risks including harmful content, cyberbullying, body image concerns, heavy use, and sleep displacement.[^surgeon-general] The American Psychological Association takes a more conditional view, but still recommends age-appropriate design, adult monitoring for younger adolescents, social media literacy, and limits that protect sleep and physical activity.[^apa-social]

The legal angle is worth noticing without turning this into an ideological fight. The United Kingdom, the European Union, and Australia have all recognized that online platforms pose specific risks to children and have tried to answer those risks through regulation. The UK's Online Safety Act places child-safety duties on regulated online services.[^online-safety] The EU's Digital Services Act has child-protection rules and 2025 guidance focused on risks such as grooming, harmful content, addictive or problematic behavior, cyberbullying, and harmful commercial practices.[^eu-minors] Australia has gone further with a social-media minimum-age framework requiring many platforms to take reasonable steps to prevent under-16s from having accounts.[^australia-age]

I also know Baroness Joanna Shields, who served as a UK Minister for Internet Safety and Security and has been associated with child online-safety efforts including WeProtect and age-verification work.[^shields] I mention that because this is not an abstract policy topic to me. People who have spent serious time inside technology, government, and child-safety work have been warning about the same basic issue: social media is not just another classroom tool.

The United States has a different legal and political environment, and I am not trying to make this a partisan argument or a proposal for a specific U.S. law. As parents here, the practical responsibility is still ours: monitor our own children's usage, delay social media access, understand the business model, and distinguish creative computing from products designed around social ranking, identity exposure, and engagement capture.

The practical lesson for schools is simple: do not treat "technology" and "social media" as synonyms. A child needs technology literacy. A child does not need social media access in order to become technologically literate.

## AI Makes Computer Fluency More Important, Not Less

The education conversation around AI is often fear-based. Some of that fear is understandable. Schools need academic-integrity policies. Students need to do their own thinking. Teachers need to know whether work reflects learning or automation.

But reducing AI to "cheating" misses the larger trend. AI is becoming a normal interface for work. In my own professional work, agentic tools changed materially over the last six months, and that direction is not going away. Students do not need unrestricted access to every AI product. They do need age-appropriate fluency with the basics:

- typing clearly and quickly;
- giving precise written instructions;
- breaking problems into steps;
- revising drafts;
- checking sources;
- identifying hallucinations and bias;
- protecting private information;
- comparing outputs;
- citing what they use;
- knowing when human judgment is required.

Typing deserves special emphasis. Phones and tablets do not build the same fluency. The core AI interface is still language: writing instructions, asking better questions, comparing responses, editing drafts, and explaining what changed. Prompting is not magic. It is structured writing, decomposition, critique, and revision. Those are school skills.

California's 2025 AI guidance explicitly frames the school task as both "learning with AI" and "learning about AI." It also defines agentic/autonomous AI and emphasizes human-centered use, privacy, academic integrity, creativity, critical thinking, and responsible implementation in TK-12 settings.[^cde-ai] UNESCO similarly frames students not just as users of AI, but as responsible co-creators who need human-centered, ethical, application, and system-design competencies.[^unesco-ai]

The workforce signal points the same direction. The World Economic Forum identifies AI and big data, networks and cybersecurity, and technology literacy among the fastest-growing skill areas through 2030.[^wef] Microsoft describes a shift toward human-AI agent teams and employees who build, delegate to, and manage agents.[^microsoft] McKinsey reports broad organizational AI adoption and substantial experimentation with AI agents.[^mckinsey]

Schools do not have to chase hype to respond to this. They can make common-sense choices: teach typing, writing, source checking, privacy, computing basics, and responsible use of tools that students will encounter outside school.

## Privacy and Containment Are First-Class Safety Issues

Another reason "screen hours" is too crude: it ignores exposure, security, and containment.

I want my children to have a high level of privacy. That means limiting where their names, photos, school affiliations, schedules, locations, peer groups, interests, and behavioral data are stored, shared, tagged, indexed, or made searchable.

This is not only about social media. Even well-intentioned school communication tools can circulate more information and more photos of children than some parents are comfortable with. The question is not just "how many minutes did a child look at a screen?" It is:

- What personal data left the family or classroom?
- Who can see it?
- Can it be copied?
- How long does it persist?
- Is it connected to an account, identity, or social graph?
- Can the child control it later?

The FTC's COPPA guidance treats children's personal information broadly, including identifiers, photos, videos, audio, geolocation, and persistent identifiers.[^ftc-coppa] The U.S. Department of Education's student privacy guidance similarly treats student personally identifiable information as both direct identifiers and indirect identifiers that can trace a student's identity.[^ed-pii] School-maintained photos and videos can also become education records under FERPA depending on context.[^ed-ferpa]

That is a different safety problem than raw minutes.

## Advertising Incentives Should Be Part of the Risk Model

From the advertising side, I would look at incentives.

Children and teenagers can be valuable to platforms even when they are not the direct buyer. A company can advertise safety tools, family features, parental controls, educational value, and "age-appropriate" experiences to parents because parental comfort is part of the path to teen adoption, retention, and normalization.

Meta's own newsroom has described parent-, caregiver-, and family-facing campaigns promoting Instagram family tools and teen supervision features.[^meta-family] Meta has also said that teens are less equipped than adults to make decisions about how their data is used for advertising, while still allowing teen ad delivery based on age and location after additional restrictions.[^meta-ads]

That does not make every safety feature fake. It does mean parent-facing safety marketing should not be confused with a neutral child-development recommendation.

The FTC has also warned about "stealth advertising" to children in digital media, where advertising is blended into games, influencer content, social feeds, video, and immersive environments.[^ftc-stealth] This is exactly why device type, business model, and interface design matter.

## Consolidation Makes Containment Harder

Media conglomeration and acquisitions add another layer.

I have seen the landscape shift from within. Messaging, social networking, games, video, advertising, analytics, commerce, identity, and now AI have become less separate over time. A parent may think they are approving one app or one device, but the business model often spans a larger ecosystem of owned properties, acquired products, shared identity systems, ad networks, recommendation systems, and data partnerships.

That matters for children because containment becomes harder when media, communication, entertainment, advertising, and identity are connected behind the scenes.

The FTC's 2024 report on social media and video streaming services found broad data collection, targeted advertising incentives, inadequate safeguards for children and teens, and competition implications when firms accumulate significant user data.[^ftc-screens] The FTC and DOJ's 2023 merger guidelines likewise call out platform markets, trends toward concentration, entrenchment of dominance, and series of acquisitions as merger-review concerns.[^merger-guidelines]

Parents and schools do not need to become antitrust lawyers. But they should understand that "one more app" is not always one more isolated container.

## What I Would Ask Schools To Do

I would not ask schools to remove technology from classrooms. I would ask them to be more precise.

Use more:

- laptops and desktops with keyboards;
- typing practice;
- writing and revision tools;
- coding and automation;
- music, art, video, and design software;
- spreadsheets and data work;
- accessibility tools;
- age-appropriate AI literacy;
- source-checking and citation exercises;
- privacy and security instruction.

Use less:

- phones during school;
- private small-screen use;
- passive streaming;
- autoplay;
- mobile games;
- social media;
- public posting;
- unsupervised chat;
- ad-driven platforms;
- tools that unnecessarily expose student identity, photos, contacts, or behavioral data.

Review more:

- classroom technology contracts;
- recurring software subscriptions;
- student-data practices;
- ad exposure;
- autoplay and recommendation defaults;
- total screen use by grade level;
- whether a tool replaces hands-on learning or enables real creative work.

I agree with the instinct to keep phones and watches contained during the school day, restrict device use during passing periods, lunch, and recess, and question whether every student needs a one-to-one device for every classroom task. I also support asking for a real cost-benefit review of school technology spending. Those are sensible governance moves.

But the school's use of filtering, monitoring, and phone containment systems should not end the discussion. They address some access and distraction risks. They do not answer the deeper questions about what kind of technology use we are encouraging, what skills we are building, what data is leaving the classroom, and whether students are learning to create or merely consume.

## My Short Answer

If a school form needs a number, I would select **more than two hours per day**.

But the real answer is:

More creative, supervised, private, tool-based computer time. Less phone time, less passive streaming, less social media, less algorithmic engagement, and stricter boundaries around student identity and data.

The goal should not be less technology. The goal should be better technology use: more creation than consumption, more tools than feeds, more keyboards than touchscreens, more judgment than panic, and more containment than exposure.
